Sound Engineering

Sound Engineering involves using technical equipment to record, mix, and reproduce music, voices, and sound effects. Sound engineers work in various industries, including music, film, television, and radio.

Tasks:

  • Set up, test, and adjust recording equipment for recording sessions and live performances
  • Record speech, music, and other sounds onto digital audio or videotape
  • Operate and maintain equipment, such as microphones, sound speakers, video screens, projectors, video monitors, and recording equipment
  • Convert video and audio recordings into digital formats for editing or archiving

Skills:

  • Active Listening - Giving full attention to what other people are saying, taking time to understand the points being made, asking questions as appropriate, and not interrupting at inappropriate times
  • Operation Monitoring - Watching gauges, dials, or other indicators to make sure a machine is working properly
  • Quality Control Analysis - Conducting tests and inspections of products, services, or processes to evaluate quality or performance

Knowledge:

  • Fine Arts - Knowledge of the theory and techniques required to compose, produce, and perform works of music, dance, visual arts, drama, and sculpture
  • Computers and Electronics - Knowledge of electronic equipment, and computer hardware and software, including applications and programming
  • Physics - Knowledge and prediction of physical principles, laws, their interrelationships, and applications to understanding fluid, material, and atmospheric dynamics, and mechanical, electrical, atomic and sub-atomic structures and processes

Sound engineers typically work full time and may work long hours during recording sessions. They often work in studios or on location for events. A bachelor's degree in audio engineering or a related field is typically required to enter this occupation.

Artistic/Conventional Workers

Artistic/Conventional workers include a wide range of occupations that involve creativity, artistic expression, and manual skills. These workers may be involved in crafts, visual arts, performing arts, and other creative endeavors.

Tasks:

  • Create original artwork, crafts, or music
  • Perform in front of live audiences, such as musicians, actors, or dancers
  • Produce and sell handmade goods or artwork
  • Teach others about art, music, or crafts

Skills:

  • Creativity - Developing ideas and turning them into reality through artistic expression
  • Fine Arts - Knowledge of the theory and techniques required to compose, produce, and perform works of music, dance, visual arts, drama, and sculpture
  • Manual Dexterity - The ability to use your hands skillfully and manipulate small objects with precision

Knowledge:

  • Artistic Techniques - Knowledge of techniques and principles involved in producing works of art, music, dance, or drama
  • Customer and Personal Service - Knowledge of principles and processes for providing customer and personal services. This includes customer needs assessment, meeting quality standards for services, and evaluation of customer satisfaction
  • Design - Knowledge of design techniques, tools, and principles involved in production of precision technical plans, blueprints, drawings, and models

Artistic/Conventional workers often work irregular hours, including evenings and weekends, to accommodate performances, shows, or sales. They may work independently or as part of a team. Formal education requirements vary depending on the specific occupation, but many artistic/conventional workers develop their skills through on-the-job training or formal education programs.
